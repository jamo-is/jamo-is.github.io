Overview

California Revealed is a statewide digitization initiative which helps public libraries, archives, museums, historical societies, and other heritage groups digitize, preserve, and provide online access to archival materials documenting the state's histories, arts, and cultures. It also provides support and assistance for cataloging, community archiving, and K-12 curriculum development, and provides training and support for Memory Labs at public libraries across California. It began in 2010 as CAVPP as a temporary program funded by the California State Library, and since then has grown in capability, scope, and demand year after year.

As Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager, Guadalupe Martinez manages and oversees California Revealed projects from application review to long-term preservation. This involves coordinating with partner organizations to write basic metadata for and then ship their collections, which are then processed, documented, and given further metadata before being sent to third-party vendors for digitization. Once digitized, they are checked for fixity and to see if metadata has been assigned correctly, and Guadalupe and other preservation managers will communicate with the digitization vendor in the case of any discrepancies. The initial quality control report is then sent to partners to be signed off, but this is not always done as familiarity with QC varies from organization to organization.

If requested, partners will then be given a physical hard drive of their digitized materials. Digitized collections are finally given three points of access, one being access copies stored on an AWS web server, and the other two being LTO tape copies with preservation quality files kept on them, one of which is kept at California Revealed in Sacramento, and the other of which is sent to a vault in Ohio. Throughout this process Guadalupe consults partners, preservation experts, vendors, and third-party services to resolve issues, establish trust, and explain A/V preservation at various levels.

Typical Tasks

As Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager, Guadalupe is responsible for assessing the feasibility of intaking a digitization project from community heritage group partners, adapting the partner’s initial metadata to California Revealed’s formatting standards, and coordinating the digitization and preservation of the materials with an external vendor. In addition to project management, Guadalupe creates educational resources such as handbooks to help demystify the digitization process and the jargon surrounding it for their community in an effort to not only help them be better stewards of their materials but to also help gain their trust as partners.

One specialty she performs as Manager is establishing a priority of care for communities based on FEMA Natural Risk Index for Natural Hazards and Healthy Places Index (HPI) scores. Such factors that are taken into account for FEMA results are the risks for climate disaster, while HPI is concerned with employment and education opportunities, air quality and access to clean water, among others impacting life expectancy. Guadalupe emphasized the importance of providing “more access to people who are already being under-resourced in other aspects of their lives, not just from a historical standpoint.” Collecting this information is vital for better understanding their preservation needs and level of impact California Revealed can have.

Technical Skill Snapshop

Audiovisual archivists must have a comprehensive understanding of the history, conservation, restoration, access, and curation of audiovisual formats, including film, magnetic video and audiotape, optical media, and digital media. According to the Association of Moving Image Archivists, the position of audiovisual digitization and preservation manager would be categorized as a mid-level position within the media archiving field. The position entails coordinating digitization endeavors and project management through collaborating with partners and vendors.

Guadalupe’s introduction to media preservation involved familiarizing themselves with and digitizing several video formats while working with the Bay Area video coalition (BAVC). At BAVC, Guadalupe undertook projects that presented them with the task of handling, cleaning, and converting footage from a wide array of video formats including open real ¼ inch tape, VHS, U-matic, DAT, and Maddox, as well as digitizing sound recordings. Acquiring this skill-set provided Guadalupe with a knowledge of and facility with audiovisual materials that they could utilize when pivoting to their current position as audiovisual digitization and preservation manager.

As preservation manager at California Revealed, Guadalupe is responsible for properly rehousing the various collections that the organization decides to nominate for digitization. This process involves connecting partners’ collections to appropriate vendors who then do the digitizing of the materials. Redirecting their knowledge of proper digitization practices and preservation protocol, Guadalupe administers the transferring of their partners’ materials and metadata, functioning as an intermediary to make these materials pertaining to California cultural history widely and freely available.

Labor Trends

One tendency of long-term preservation initiatives is the uncertainty and precarious nature of funding. In the case of California Revealed, they began as an initiative of the California State Library and have been primarily funded by them for their 15 year history. Long-term preservation projects like California Revealed find themselves at odds with yearly, fiscally-sponsored budgets, especially at a time when libraries have found themselves politicized and subject to sudden federal cuts like last year’s cancellation of Institute of Museum and Library Science grants. While the State Library’s IMLS funding is being restored after the cuts were struck down in court late last year, time constraints and deliverables required by the State Library still stand, meaning the next year’s worth of California Revealed’s projects must be completed in just eight months and meet requirements set when funds were awarded.

These constraints often entail very strict ways in which funds can be appropriated, and can be imposed with a limited understanding of digital preservation work, severely limiting the autonomy of organizations like California Revealed. For example, having bespoke storage systems set up specifically for California Revealed digital repository isn’t practical due to this fiscal funding structure, and therefore they use Amazon Web Services and Amazon Simple Storage Service in combination with the digital asset management system Archipelago. California Revealed has navigated funding uncertainty and institutional constraints by recently registering as a 501 (c) 3 nonprofit, enabling them to apply for and accept funding from outside sources.

While funding sources for long-term preservation work are limited, California Revealed has been awarded grants from the Mellon Foundation and elsewhere, enabling them to continue their mission as well as expand their scope into research and education. This is not typically the case for archives who lose funding or become acquired by another institution. In most cases, these archives lose the ability to negotiate the life of their materials, be it via deaccessioning or third party contractors returning or discarding or anything which can’t be paid for. As a growing media repository, California Revealed also has the added burden of needing to explain not only their unique partner model but also their various audiovisual, born digital, and print specific preservation needs to larger funding institutions normally accustomed to typical GLAM institutions.
